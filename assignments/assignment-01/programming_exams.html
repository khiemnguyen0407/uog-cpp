A breakdown of assessment methods at Harvard, Stanford, and MIT

It is a common misconception that top-tier computer science programs have moved entirely to digital, IDE-assisted coding. In reality, the "pen and paper" method is seeing a resurgence.

Writing code on paper forces you to "be the compiler" and mentally trace logic without digital crutches.

The Philosophy: Why Paper?

Fundamental Mastery

Without a debugger or auto-complete, students must internalize memory management and syntax rules. This is especially critical for languages like C and C++.

The AI Response

With the rise of LLMs, professors use handwritten exams as a "clean room" environment to ensure the work is authentically the student's own.

Why top Schools still use paper

  • Fundamental Mastery: Writing on paper forces you to "be the compiler." You have to mentally trace your logic and memory management (especially in C/C++) without a debugger telling you where the error is.
  • The "AI Crisis": Recently, Stanford professors (like Jure Leskovec) explicitly returned to handwritten, in-person exams because it is the only way to guarantee a student isn't using AI to generate the solution.
  • Technical Interviews: Many top-tier companies (Google, Meta) still use "whiteboarding" for interviews. Paper exams prepare students for the pressure of explaining code without an IDE.

Stanford University

Stanford remains a bastion of traditional testing. Many core classes (CS103, CS107) still use paper. However, they also pioneered BlueBook, a software that mimics paper by providing a raw text editor with no internet, no syntax highlighting, and no compiler.
